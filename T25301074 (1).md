## **Abstract** 

Emotion recognition plays an important role in advancing human-computer interaction and affective computing. While multimodal systems have shown promise, most rely on static fusion strategies that fail in noisy environments. This thesis proposes a framework for Dynamic Attention-Based Multimodal Fusion designed to eventually integrate EEG, speech, and facial expressions. In this Phase 1 implementation, we establish the core Audio-Visual baseline using EfficientFace and ResNet-18 encoders, fused via a Transformer-based attention mechanism. This architecture allows the system to dynamically weigh audio and visual cues based on reliability. Future phases will integrate EEG signals to detect unexpressed emotions and validate the system on the MAHNOB-HCI dataset. Current experiments on the RAVDESS dataset demonstrate the efficacy of the attention fusion strategy, setting a robust foundation for the inclusion of physiological signals in subsequent research.

## **Introduction** 

## **1.1 Background** 

Review of literature and background study 

Emotions are complex psychological states expressed through a synchronous combination of facial expressions, vocal prosody, and physiological responses. In the domain of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), the ability to accurately decode these signals is fundamental to creating empathetic intelligent systems. Traditionally, Emotion Recognition (ER) research has operated in silos, focusing either on Computer Vision for facial analysis or Signal Processing for speech emotion recognition. The holistic nature of human affect is frequently missed by these unimodal systems, though; for example, a visual smile can convey either sarcasm or happiness depending solely on the vocal tone that goes with it. 

To address this ambiguity, Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER) has emerged as a dominant paradigm. MER systems use complementary information to increase classification accuracy by combining visual and acoustic data. Transformer-based architectures, which were initially created for Natural Language Processing, have gained attention recently in Deep Learning because of their capacity to represent distant temporal dependencies in sequential data. In order to lay the foundation for sophisticated hybrid systems, this study investigates the application of such architectures to the particular problem of Audio-Visual emotion recognition. . 

## **1.2 Motivation** 

The main reason for this research is that current static fusion models have problems and there were specific problems that came up in the early stages of experiments. Initial investigations utilizing "static" fusion strategies (such as simple feature concatenation) revealed significant fragility; these models treat all input channels as equally reliable, causing performance degradation when one modality is noisy or occluded. Furthermore, our preliminary attempts to utilize a direct Transformer-based fusion of Audio and Visual data yielded a validation accuracy of approximately 70%. While promising, this falls short of the robust performance required for real-world deployment. This "performance gap" motivates a strategic pivot in our research trajectory: establishing a solid Transformer-based baseline in Phase 1, specifically to 

1 

identify its limitations, which will drive the development of an optimized Hybrid Architecture (combining Convolutional Neural Networks with Transformers) in Phase 2. To get from our current baseline to the goal accuracy of 90%, we need to use this iterative method.. 

## **1.3 Problem Statement** 

Even though Multimodal AI has a lot of potential in theory, putting it into practice on datasets like RAVDESS is hard because of a number of technical issues: 

1. **Misalignment of Features:** Audio data (temporal spectrograms) and visual data (spatial images) are in completely different feature spaces. A major architectural challenge is still merging them with a Transformer without causing "negative transfer," which is when the noise from one modality messes up the other. 

2. **The "Pure Transformer" Limitation:** While Transformers excel at global context, they lack the inductive bias required to extract low-level local features from small datasets. This results in the "70% plateau" observed in our preliminary work, where the model struggles to refine local spatial details without CNN assistance. 

3. **Data Scarcity and Overfitting:** The RAVDESS dataset is not very big compared to other datasets in the field. Complex attention-based models are likely to overfit, which means they remember actors instead of applying what they learned to new subjects. 

## **1.4 Objective** 

The central objective of this research is to develop a high-performance Multimodal Emotion Recognition system that progresses from a Transformer-based baseline to an optimized Hybrid architecture. 

## **1.4.1 General Objective** 

To design, implement, and validate a dynamic fusion framework for Audio-Visual emotion recognition that targets a classification accuracy of 90% through iterative architectural optimization. 

## **1.4.2 Specific Objectives** 

- To use the most advanced feature extractors: EfficientFace for visual spatial features and ResNet-18 for acoustic spectrograms. 

- To create a Transformer-based Intermediate Attention (IA) module that weighs audio and visual inputs based on how reliable they are in context. 

- To set a strong P1 baseline accuracy of 70% on the RAVDESS dataset, which shows that the attention mechanism works.

- To propose and design a Hybrid Architecture (Phase 2) that integrates CNN blocks into the Transformer pipeline to improve local feature retention and reach the 90% accuracy target.

This study employs a phased implementation strategy, emphasizing the development of a resilient Audio-Visual infrastructure prior to advancing to intricate optimization.

## **1.5.1 Current Implementation of Transformer-Based Fusion** 

The system uses a dual-stream pipeline to process the RAVDESS dataset: 

## **Visual Stream** 

MTCNN processes raw video to find faces. A distributed sampling strategy picks 15 frames from each video and sends them through an EfficientFace encoder (trained on AffectNet) to get spatial feature embeddings. 

## **Audio Stream** 

The ResNet-18 encoder processes audio tracks by turning them into Log-Mel Spectrograms. This makes audio signals look like 2D images, which lets the model pick up on acoustic textures. 

## **Fusion** 

The feature vectors from both streams are put into a single 128-dimensional space. A Multi-Head Transformer uses attention mechanisms to combine these features. It learns which modality to focus on for each sample in real time. 

## **1.5.2 Proposed Hybrid Optimization** 

Phase 2 will involve replacing some Transformer layers with Convolutional blocks to make a Hybrid CNN-Transformer network. This will build on the current 70% baseline and is designed to address the feature alignment problems noted in the Problem Statement.

- **Modality:** Phase 1 focuses on combining audio and video; EEG integration is not part of this work.

## **2.1 Preliminaries** 

## **2.1.1 Foundation of Affective Computing** 

The goal of affective computing is to make machines able to identify, understand, and respond to human emotions in a way that makes sense in the context and is true to how people feel.1 The primary difficulty in this undertaking is the absence of a universal agreement on the definition and classification of "emotion." Discrete Emotion Theory, prominently advocated by Paul Ekman, asserts that humans have a finite array of fundamental emotions that are biologically inherent and universally acknowledged across various cultures. These are usually happiness, sadness, wrath, fear, disgust, and surprise.3 This theory posits that emotion recognition is a multi-class classification problem from a computational perspective. Every emotion is regarded as a separate category characterized by certain physiological and behavioral indicators. For example, the "happiness" category is linked to certain facial muscle contractions (Action Unit 12) and changes in vocal pitch.7 This approach makes sense and fits with how people name emotions, but it doesn’t always show how complicated "blended" emotions are or how different intensities of a single affective state might be. Some people say that seeing emotions as separate boxes doesn’t take into account how they might change from one state to another, such as going from being irritated to being angry.3. In affective computing, a critical distinction is made between "expressed" emotion (facial expressions, speech) and "felt" emotion (physiological signals). Expressed emotion is largely controlled by the somatic nervous system and is susceptible to voluntary manipulation, social masking, and cultural display rules. Physiological signals, particularly EEG, are driven by the autonomic nervous system and deep-seated brain structures that are significantly more difficult to control voluntarily.15 EEG captures the immediate neurophysiological response to an affective stimulus, providing a more "objective" measure of the internal state.15 This "felt" emotion acts as a ground-truth anchor in MER systems. For instance, while a user might mask their frustration with a polite smile, their EEG patterns in the Beta and Gamma bands may reveal high arousal and negative valence, allowing a multimodal system to resolve the ambiguity that a unimodal visual system would miss. 

## **2.1.2 Deep Learning for Feature Extraction** 

The effectiveness of any Pattern Recognition system is fundamentally constrained by the quality of its feature representation. In the past, emotion recognition depended 

5 

on "handcrafted" features, which are descriptors that were made by hand using knowledge of the field. For the visual part, this meant using techniques like Local Binary Patterns (LBP) or Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) to find facial features. Low-level prosodic elements including pitch, vigor, and Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) were also important for recognizing emotions in sound. Even while these methods set significant standards, they often weren’t strong enough to handle noise in the environment and didn’t pick up on high-level semantic abstractions. Deep Learning has changed the way we think about things from human feature engineering to automated Representation Learning. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are now the standard for this purpose since they can learn hierarchical feature maps without depending on language. Visual Encoding: New architectures use deep CNNs to make spatial maps out of facial emotions. Older works used heavy architectures like VGG-16, whereas newer ones focus on making things work quickly in real time. EfficientFace and MobileNet are two architectures that use depth-wise separable convolutions to pick up on little micro-expressions. This is much cheaper than regular 3D-CNNs [Zhao et al.]. Audio Encoding: A big change in the audio field recently has been going from 1D signal processing to 2D image-based analysis. Researchers can use strong 2D-CNN backbones like ResNet18 on acoustic data by turning raw audio waveforms into Log-Mel Spectrograms [Cite ResNet publication]. This method incorporates time and frequency as spatial axes, which lets the model capture subtle acoustic textures (such the "roughness" of an angry voice) that are commonly missed in 1D temporal analysis. **2.1.3 Multimodal Fusion Strategies** 

The main problem with Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER) is that once characteristics are taken from separate modalities, Fusion is the process of combining different types of data streams into one. Literature often classifies fusion methods into three different paradigms: Early Fusion (Feature-Level): This method combines raw feature vectors from several modalities into one high-dimensional vector before sending data to a classifier. Early Fusion is fast on computers, but it has the "curse of dimensionality" and presupposes that auditory and visual elements are fully in sync, which is not always true in real life [Cite general fusion paper]. Late Fusion (Decision-Level): In this method, each modality has its own independent classifier trained, and their final probability scores are aggregated using voting techniques like Averaging or Majority Vote. Late Fusion stops one noisy modality from messing up the learning process of the other, but it doesn’t pick up on correlations between modalities. For instance, it cannot model the complex relationship where a smile (visual) changes the interpretation of a sarcastic tone (audio). Hybrid and Attention-Based Fusion: The field has moved toward Dynamic Fusion techniques to get around the problems with static weighting. Inspired by the Transformer architecture [Cite Vaswani et al.], recent frameworks employ Attention Mechanisms to weigh modalities adaptively. Unlike static fusion, which assigns fixed importance to Audio and Video, Attention-based models (such as the Hybrid CNN-Transformer) can dynamically focus on the most reliable signal frame-by-frame. For example, if the face is occluded, the model automatically shifts its "attention" weights to the audio stream. This ability to dynamically re-weight is essential for ensuring robustness in "in-the-wild" situations and serves as the theoretical foundation for the architecture suggested in this thesis by Bouali et al. ## **2.2 Review of Existing Research** 

Babu et al. [7] developed AffectiveFusionNet, a cutting-edge hybrid architecture that integrates Visual Transformers (ViTs) with Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to tackle the intricacies of human emotions. With the help of the self-attention mechanism of ViTs, the model is able to capture global contextual information and subtle facial cues. On the other hand, VAE provides a framework for encoding high-dimensional multimodal data into a compressed latent space. A distinguishing feature of this work is its integration of graph-based relational learning. This is inspired by the COGMEN framework. Furthermore, the architecture incorporates a hierarchical attention mechanism adapted from the V2EM model to process audiovisual streams with precision. The findings indicate that the integration of global contextual awareness through Transformers and structured latent representation via VAEs establishes a superior benchmark for intricate human-computer interaction scenarios. 

Avots et al. [8] explains the limitations of traditional emotion recognition systems when transitioning from controlled environments to ’real-world’ scenarios or as mentioned by the authors as "in-the-wild". Naturally, we are surrounded by background noise, varying subjects, and occlusions. The authors emphasize that while facial expressions have historically dominated the literature, but solely depending on a single modality is not sufficient. Their research tests the performance of audio-visual models that are trained on specific datasets, against AFEW (Acted Facial Expressions in the Wild) database. The study advocates for a more balanced integration of prosodic speech parameters and visual cues by comparing algorithmic performance with human decision-makers. This highlights the urgent need for more robust fusion strategies that take into account the diversity of human ethnicity, culture, and environmental interference. 

Soleymani et al. [9] executed a seminal investigation on continuous emotion recognition by examining the synchronization and cross-modality interference between EEG data and facial expressions. This study use the Valence-Arousal-Dominance (VAD) dimensional model to provide continuous emotional profiles, as opposed to systems that focus on distinct emotional labels. To capture the temporal relationships of "felt" emotions, the researchers employed a Long Short-Term Memory Recurrent Neural Network (LSTM-RNN) to extract Power Spectral Density (PSD) from EEG and face landmarks as features. One of the research’s contributions is looking into whether EEG affective information is real or just a byproduct of EMG activity in face muscles. Granger causality study indicates that physiological signals provide unique information applicable in contexts without facial cues, despite the significant impact of facial expressions on EEG data.This study emphasizes the significance of temporal modeling in multimodal systems and elucidates the difficulties posed by cross-modal artifacts in physiological sensing. 

Xing et al. [10] examined the interplay between exterior multimedia stimuli and internal physiological responses by creating a multimodal framework that combines EEG signals with audio-visual elements. This study utilizes EEG as a definitive measure of "internal" emotional arousal, so addressing the limitation of relying just on outward emotions, which can be intentionally obscured. The authors utilized Differential Entropy (DE) features across multiple frequency bands to interpret EEG data, while employing a 3D Convolutional Neural Network (C3D) to extract spatial 

7 

and temporal information from video frames and traditional acoustic feature sets to record speech prosody. Comparing various fusion strategies is one of this work’s main contributions; it shows that a feature-level fusion approach, which integrates fused audio-visual vectors with physiological data, performs noticeably better than unimodal baselines. The research finds that adding EEG signals adds a "hidden" layer of emotional context that makes up for the vagueness that is typically present in audio-visual cues that are solely external. This leads to a substantial increase in accuracy on the DEAP and MAHNOB-HCI datasets. 

Krishna and Patil [11] proposed a multimodal emotion recognition framework that employs 1D Convolutional Neural Networks (1D-CNNs) and Cross-Modal Attention to process both text and raw waveforms. Using an end-to-end 1D-CNN encoder that extracts high-level features from raw audio signals instead of manually made spectrogram features is a big change from how things are usually done. The authors developed a cross-modal attention mechanism to link the significance of various modalities. This method allows the model to capture complex inter-modal connections by allowing audio features to pay attention to text sequences and vice versa. The cross-modal attention strategy achieved cutting-edge results on the IEMOCAP dataset, and the research indicates that processing raw waveforms enhances performance compared to spectrogram-based models. This study demonstrates the efficacy of attention-based fusion over simple feature concatenation by illustrating how attention facilitates profound interactions among disparate sequences. Xue et al. [12] presented the Multi-Tensor Fusion Network (MTFN) featuring a Hybrid Attention architecture to tackle the issues of feature redundancy and inter-modal dynamics in sentiment analysis. The framework employs Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (Bi-LSTM) networks to obtain contextual representations within distinct modalities, subsequently processed via a hybrid attention mechanism that integrates both self-attention and cross-modal attention. This dual-attention method helps the model pay attention to both essential unimodal characteristics and the links between multiple data streams. The authors employed a multi-tensor fusion technique to efficiently integrate these attended features. This technique puts the interactions in a high-dimensional environment while retaining the integrity of both bimodal and trimodal linkages. The MTFN effectively reduces the effects of redundant information, as shown by experiments on the MOSI and MOSEI datasets. This makes it perform better than traditional concatenation and simple attention models. 

Li et al. [13] addressed the pragmatic issue of insufficient data and fragmented signals by proposing a Joint Learning framework for multimodal emotion identification in the absence of specific modalities. The study presents an innovative methodology that enables simultaneous training of models on many heterogeneous datasets, even without a cohesive sensor configuration (for example, integrating a video-only dataset with an EEG-speech dataset). The authors used a Modality-Specific Encoder architecture to combine different inputs into a single feature space. They also used a Joint Learning Strategy that takes advantage of shared emotional knowledge across different corpora. One of the best new things about this work is that it can still get high recognition accuracy during inference even when one or more modalities aren’t available. This helps keep performance from dropping as much as it does in most fusion models. This research creates a strong foundation for putting emotion recognition systems into action in unpredictable, real-world situations by 

8 

combining different types of data and dealing with "severely missing" modalities. While traditional feature extraction methods laid the initial groundwork for emotion recognition, deep learning has fundamentally shifted the paradigm from manual feature engineering to automated representation learning. In the visual domain, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have become the dominant architecture for facial expression analysis. Early systems relied on deep models such as VGG-16 and AlexNet; however, recent research emphasizes lightweight networks optimized for real-time deployment. Zhao et al. demonstrated that depth-wise separable convolutions, as implemented in architectures such as MobileNet and EfficientFace, achieve competitive accuracy for facial expression recognition while significantly reducing computational complexity [15]. 

Parallel advances have occurred in the acoustic domain. Historically, Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) relied on handcrafted descriptors such as pitch and energy processed by traditional classifiers. Contemporary research increasingly treats audio classification as an image recognition task by converting raw waveforms into Melspectrograms. He et al. successfully applied the ResNet architecture to spectrogram representations, showing that residual skip connections enable hierarchical acoustic feature learning while mitigating vanishing gradient issues [5]. This cross-domain use of CNNs facilitates the integration of audio and visual modalities into a shared embedding space, forming a critical foundation for multimodal fusion. 

The performance of a Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER) system is largely determined by its fusion strategy—the mechanism used to combine heterogeneous data streams. Existing literature generally categorizes fusion approaches into Early Fusion, Late Fusion, and Model-Level Fusion [25]. Early Fusion, or feature-level fusion, concatenates raw feature vectors from different modalities before classification. While computationally efficient, Kalateh et al. report that this method often suffers from the curse of dimensionality and fails to capture asynchronous temporal dynamics between audio and visual signals [25]. Late Fusion, or decision-level fusion, independently trains classifiers for each modality and aggregates their predictions. Although this prevents any single modality from dominating the learning process, it fails to capture fine-grained inter-modal correlations—such as mismatches between facial expressions and vocal prosody—that are critical for decoding complex emotional states [25]. 

To overcome the limitations of static weighting schemes, recent studies have introduced dynamic fusion mechanisms based on attention. Inspired by the Transformer architecture proposed by Vaswani et al., attention mechanisms have been adapted for MER to assign time-varying importance weights to each modality [7]. John and Kawanishi introduced cross-modal attention, enabling the model to selectively emphasize reliable modalities under noisy conditions, significantly improving robustness compared to static fusion approaches [19]. 

The most recent evolution in MER research is the development of hybrid architectures that combine the spatial feature extraction capabilities of CNNs with the temporal modeling strengths of Transformers. While pure Transformer-based models excel at capturing long-range dependencies, they often lack the inductive bias needed to extract low-level visual and acoustic patterns from relatively small emotion datasets such as RAVDESS [9], leading to overfitting and unstable convergence. To address this limitation, Chen et al. proposed a multi-stage dynamical fusion framework in which CNN backbones generate modality-specific embeddings that are sub- 

9 

sequently processed through multi-head self-attention layers for temporal modeling [20]. This hybrid design stabilizes training by leveraging CNN-based inductive priors while allowing Transformers to capture global dependencies across multimodal sequences. Such architectures directly motivate the methodology adopted in this thesis, which shifts from pure Transformer models toward CNN–Transformer hybrids for improved generalization on limited datasets. Chen et al. addressed a critical limitation in Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER): the lack of datasets that simultaneously capture high-quality external behavioral cues (audio and video) and internal physiological responses (EEG and EMG) [17]. In their study, _Emotion Recognition With Audio, Video, EEG, and EMG: A Dataset and Baseline Approaches_ , the authors introduced the EAV dataset to examine the complementary nature of heterogeneous modalities. Their motivation was to mitigate the ambiguity problem inherent in unimodal systems. Facial expressions and vocal prosody are subject to social masking, whereas physiological signals such as EEG and EMG are involuntary and difficult to manipulate. By recording participants equipped with EEG headsets and EMG sensors while viewing emotionally evocative video clips, the study created a controlled environment for evaluating whether physiological signals could correct misclassifications made by audio-visual models. This work builds upon earlier physiological datasets such as DEAP [2] and MAHNOB-HCI [3], but distinguishes itself through synchronized high-resolution audio-visual and physiological recordings, making it well suited for deep learning pipelines. A major contribution of this study is its systematic benchmarking of fusion strategies. The authors compared feature-level fusion, which concatenates raw feature vectors, with decision-level fusion, which aggregates classifier outputs. Their experiments demonstrated that fusing all four modalities consistently outperformed unimodal and bimodal systems, with feature-level fusion yielding superior results [17]. However, the reliance on Support Vector Machines and static fusion weights limits adaptability under noisy conditions. This limitation directly motivates the use of attention-based fusion mechanisms in this thesis. 

The development of robust emotion recognition systems is fundamentally dependent on the availability of high-quality annotated datasets. In Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER), datasets serve not only as training corpora but also as standardized benchmarks for validating generalization performance. As architectures evolve toward hybrid deep learning frameworks, datasets capturing synchronized audiovisual and physiological signals have become increasingly essential. For Phase 1 of this research, the primary requirement is a dataset offering high-fidelity, synchronized audio and video recordings. The Ryerson Audio-Visual Database of Emotional Speech and Song (RAVDESS) fulfills this requirement through its controlled recording conditions and lexically matched utterances [9]. Its clean audiovisual structure makes it particularly suitable for training deep encoders such as EfficientFace and ResNet without the confounding influence of environmental noise, enabling precise evaluation of attention-based fusion mechanisms. In contrast, the Interactive Emotional Dyadic Motion Capture (IEMOCAP) database emphasizes spontaneous dyadic interactions across approximately twelve hours of audiovisual recordings [1]. While this introduces greater variability and noise, it provides a more naturalistic benchmark for evaluating generalization performance and robustness in later experimental phases. 

To support the transition toward hybrid architectures incorporating EEG (Phase 

10 

2), physiological datasets must be considered. The DEAP dataset records EEG responses from participants watching music videos and labels emotions using the valence-arousal-dominance (VAD) dimensional framework [2]. Similarly, the MAHNOBHCI dataset integrates synchronized facial video, audio, and physiological signals, enabling multimodal fusion studies under controlled conditions [3]. The SEED dataset further extends physiological benchmarking by addressing cross-session variability, capturing EEG recordings from the same participants across multiple days to highlight the non-stationary nature of neural signals [4]. These datasets collectively form the foundation for investigating the integration of physiological signals into multimodal emotion recognition systems. 

Recent MER research has increasingly adopted Transformer-based fusion and endto-end deep learning architectures, moving beyond static feature concatenation. Three influential studies from 2022 illustrate this shift and directly inform the architectural choices of this thesis. Bouali et al. proposed a cross-modal deep learning framework designed to model spatio-temporal dependencies in acted speech [16]. John and Kawanishi introduced a multimodal Transformer architecture employing modality-specific self-attention and cross-modal attention branches [19]. Hussain et al. proposed an end-to-end framework that learns directly from raw waveforms and pixel-level inputs, rather than relying on handcrafted descriptors [18]. These findings support the adoption of deep encoders such as ResNet-18 and EfficientFace in this thesis to enable representation learning directly from raw data. 

## **2.3 Summary of Key Findings** 

## **2.3.1 Decoding the EEG-Facial Expression Paradox** 

The "Affective Gap" refers to the mismatch between an individual’s subjective internal experience and their observable behaviour [23]. Physiological signals, specifically EEG, are considered more objective and reliable because they are not influenced by subjective human will [31]. EEG allows for the measurement of "induced" or "felt" emotions rather than just "perceived" or "posed" expressions. By fusing these modalities, researchers can identify when a facial expression is incongruent with the underlying neural state—a phenomenon common in masked emotions. For example, research utilising the DEAP and MAHNOB-HCI datasets demonstrates that combining facial video frames with EEG signals enhances the stability of the emotion network and significantly improves cross-subject classification [31]. To extract meaningful information from the "noisy" EEG signal, two primary frequency-domain features are utilized: Power Spectral Density (PSD) and Differential Entropy (DE). While PSD is the traditional mainstream method, DE has emerged as a more potent feature for emotion classification. On the SEED dataset, DE features have achieved accuracies that are 7%–19% higher than PSD-based methods when used in conjunction with deep learning models like CNNs. 

## **2.3.2 Addressing the In-the-Wild Problem and Affective Subjectivity** 

Research by Avots et al. highlights the failures of traditional models when confronted with the subjectivity of expression in natural environments. Unlike acted 

11 

datasets, real-world data is often "incomplete" or "asymmetric" [32]. For instance, in a video sequence, the emotional state may be dynamic—a subject might transition from sadness to happiness—but a model trained on static frames will fail to capture this trajectory. Furthermore, the "expected" emotion of a video creator may differ significantly from the "induced" emotion felt by the viewer, adding a layer of subjective complexity that standard classification labels fail to represent [30]. The "Missing Modality" problem is a specific subset of this challenge. When a sensor fails (e.g., a wearable EEG cap loses connection or a user’s face is occluded), a multimodal system must be able to maintain its performance using only the remaining data. Simply dropping the modality often leads to a "catastrophic performance degradation" because the cross-modal interaction layers are interrupted [33]. 

## **2.3.3 Trend and Summary Table** 

The development of Multimodal Emotion Recognition shows a clear trend: as the models get more "interactive" and "robust," they start to work more like the human brain does when it comes to cross-validating sensory inputs. The simple feature stacking of the past has been superseded with advanced attention mechanisms that can cross the "Affective Gap" and deal with the unpredictability of real-world situations.

## **Conclusion** 

This research successfully establishes a robust framework for Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER) by transitioning from traditional static integration to a Dynamic Attention-Based Fusion paradigm. In this Phase 1 implementation, the synergy between EfficientFace for spatial facial encoding and ResNet-18 for acoustic spectrogram analysis demonstrated that high-fidelity feature extraction is critical for decoding complex human affect. The core contribution—a Transformer-based Intermediate Attention module—validated the hypothesis that dynamically weighing modalities based on their reliability can overcome the "negative transfer" common in noisy environments.The fact that the RAVDESS dataset reached the goal of 70% baseline accuracy shows that this attention mechanism works. However, the results also showed a clear "performance plateau" because pure Transformers have inductive bias problems when applied on tiny datasets. This provides an important empirical basis for the Phase 2 strategy, which calls for the development of a Hybrid CNN-Transformer architecture to achieve the 90% accuracy goal. Additionally, the literature synthesis presented here emphasizes that although audio-visual systems are proficient in capturing "expressed" emotions, the eventual incorporation of EEG signals is essential to close the "Affective Gap," enabling the system to differentiate between social masking and genuine internal states. Ultimately, this thesis provides both the architectural infrastructure and the strategic blueprint for a biologically plausible, resilient, and high-performance affective computing system
